## Step-by-Step Licensure Guide

ND has **both** NLC and APRN Compact. Single-state endorsement takes **2-4 weeks** — one of the fastest nationally.

### Step 1: Complete MSN or DNP with PMHNP Focus

Graduate from an accredited advanced practice program. Must complete **30 contact hours** of pharmacotherapy education related to your psychiatric scope.

### Step 2: Pass the ANCC PMHNP-BC Exam

Maintain active national certification.

| Membership Status | Exam Fee |
|:---|:---|
| Non-member | $395 |
| ANA member | $295 |
| AANP member | $340 |

Learn more about the [PMHNP-BC credential](/blog/what-does-pmhnp-bc-mean)

### Step 3: Apply for Your ND RN License

North Dakota is an **NLC state**. If your primary state of residence is another NLC state, your multistate RN license is valid here.

### Step 4: Apply for Your ND APRN License

Submit your APRN application to the North Dakota Board of Nursing. If your home state participates in the **APRN Compact**, your multistate APRN privilege covers ND.

### Step 5: Apply for Prescriptive Authority

Submit a **separate application** + **$75 fee** with evidence of 30 pharmacotherapy hours.

### Step 6: Register with the DEA

Apply for your federal DEA registration using your North Dakota practice address.

- **Fees:** ~$888 for 3 years

### Step 7: Apply for an NPI Number

Obtain your National Provider Identifier (NPI) via the [CMS NPPES system](https://nppes.cms.hhs.gov/) (Free).

## ND Practice Authority Details

**Current status:** North Dakota is a **Full Practice Authority** state — no transition, no collaborative agreement.

APRNs can independently assess clients, synthesize data, formulate diagnoses, and prescribe therapeutic regimens. State statute formally recognizes NPs as **primary care providers** under the medical assistance program with the same rights as primary care physicians.

**Day-to-day impact:**
You can independently sign:
- ✅ Advanced directive forms
- ✅ DNR orders
- ✅ Death certificates

## Prescriptive Authority in ND

**What can PMHNPs prescribe?**
APRNs can order medical devices, nutrition, diagnostic services, and prescribe drugs and controlled substances independently.

**Schedule II-V rules:**
Full prescribing of **Schedule II through V** controlled substances, including pharmaceutical samples.

**Requirements:**
- Separate application + $75 fee
- 30 contact hours of pharmacotherapy education
- Federal DEA registration

## Telehealth Rules in ND

**Can PMHNPs do telehealth?**
Yes — essential for ND's vast rural regions, with Medicaid reimbursement parity.

As the **first APRN Compact state**, PMHNPs with a multistate APRN license can practice telehealth in ND **without a separate license**.

**Controlled substance teleprescribing:**
Federal DEA and HHS flexibilities have been extended through **December 31, 2027**.

## Tips for PMHNPs Moving to ND

1. **Leverage the APRN Compact:** ND was the first state to enact it. If your home state also participates, practice here on your multistate privilege — no separate license needed.

2. **Track Your Practice Hours:** Renewal requires **400 practice hours** over the preceding 4 years.

3. **Audit Your CE Requirements:** Biennial renewal needs 12 CE hours, plus **15 pharmacology hours** for prescribers.

4. **Apply Separately for Prescribing:** Prescriptive authority requires its own application, $75 fee, and 30 pharmacotherapy hours.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is North Dakota a Full Practice Authority state for PMHNPs?**
A: Yes, with no transition period. NPs are recognized as primary care providers with the same rights as physicians under the medical assistance program.

**Q: Can PMHNPs in North Dakota prescribe Schedule II controlled substances?**
A: Yes, with full independence. Requires separate prescriptive authority application ($75) and DEA registration.

**Q: Is North Dakota part of the NLC and APRN Compact?**
A: Yes to both — ND was the **first state** to enact the APRN Compact (March 2021).

**Q: What are the CE requirements to renew my ND PMHNP license?**
A: Complete 12 CE hours every two years plus 400 practice hours over 4 years. Prescribers need an additional 15 pharmacology hours.
